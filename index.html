Wayfarer

Rules for science fiction roleplaying.

Manifesto

Ignore everything you know about Star Wars except for the original movie. Flush from your mind all notions of prequels or sequels or paperback novels or lackluster MMOFPS games or fan-maintained wiki drama. Poof!

Here's what matters for our purposes:

  • A decadent and corrupt intestellar empire is coming apart at the seams.
  • Scrappy, disunited freedom fighters chase their disparate ideals.
  • Cunning rogues pull off daring heists.
  • Exotic alien environments and cultures abound.
  • The promise of secret ancient power calls to those brave enough to seek it.
  • Ruthless bounty hunters dispense frontier justice.
  • Gay robots.

Recall how you felt while watching the binary sunset for the first time. Out there is a universe ripe with adventure!

That's the feeling we mean to evoke when playing Wayfarer.

Rules

Unless otherwise stated, fall back upon the 1981 edition of the classic Traveller RPG. It is the foundation upon which we will build.

Personal combat

The turn sequence is the same as the rebooted XCOM video games.

Each side chooses its characters in no particular order. Each character's turn consists of one of the following:

  • Move, then action
  • Action, then move
  • Two moves
  • An especially long action

What is a move? It's an amount of movement in meters equal to your dexterity + endurance.

What is an action? It could be an attack as described in the Traveller rules. It could be reloading a weapon. It could even be establishing an "overwatch" as in XCOM.

Ship combat

Vector combat is rad and quite straightforward. Rather than miniatures, we adjust the scale from classic Traveller so that the action fits on a piece of paper.

I'm not a fan of the giant megaships that eventually became common in Traveller. I prefer to stick with the Book 2 rules. Something about the way gravitic drives work imposes an upper limit on the size of vessels.
